How to afford a mountain bike? (Explained)

Nature Often
April 19, 2022
Updated on April 19, 2022

Mountain bikes vary greatly in quality, design, purpose, and price.

If you’re on a budget and looking for the cheapest bike possible, or if you’re wondering if building a bike is as cheap as it sounds (it’s not), or if you want to know if it’s possible to pay for a mountain bike in installments, or if you want to know what kind of pricing to expect on the cheaper end of the downhill racing bike scale, you’ve come to the right place.

How Can You Afford a Mountain Bike?

The answer to this question greatly depends on what level of quality you want your bike to be and how much disposable income you have.

Mountain bikes cost anywhere from $400 to $10,000, so before we give a definitive answer, we should narrow down what you’re looking for and what your financial constraints are. A basic bike costs $400 to $800, a mid-tier bike costs $1000 to $2500, and a truly great bike costs $3000 to $10,000.

The first place to look when your budget is severely limited is the online resale market.

It’s here that you’re most likely to find a deal from someone who just wants to get rid of an old bike and doesn’t mind if they break even or lose money on the purchase.

You can bid on online auctions or even haggle with the seller if you’re willing to be crafty.

Here’s some good news if you’re on a budget: the absolute cheapest new $400 hardtail (this means that only the front wheel has a suspension system) mountain bikes are of respectable quality.

Companies like Trek and Specialized make beginner bikes that use good materials and are assembled to be sturdy and reliable, and these bikes can even do some more advanced single-track riding if you’re willing to put up with their limitations.

If $400 is still too much for you to spare all at once, consider using a credit card, finding a bike store that offers a layaway plan, or taking advantage of a bike-specific credit union loan.

If you have a steady income but can’t afford to drop $1000 to $2000 on a new bike, the above options still apply—credit cards, layaway plans, and other loans are all possible. At this level, you can expect to pay $100 to $300 per month for several months and afford a great mid-level piece of equipment with a full suspension.

How Can You Afford a New Mountain Bike?

Some bike stores offer layaway plans, and you can contact them to ask about this option. Basic credit cards typically have a credit limit of around $500 to $1000, and this range would cover a sold low-to-mid-tier mountain bike if you want to pay for it over several months.

A credit card with a larger, let’s say, $5000 credit limit could net you a similar deal for a higher-end bike if you’re willing to make monthly payments for a longer time. Again, credit unions are known for offering bike loans, which is a great option that only suffers from the limitation that you must already be a member of a credit union to take out a loan.

You can strategize your purchase by simply looking at your monthly income and picking an amount that you’re able to subtract from it while still meeting your other costs.

Always make sure that you take note of the interest rate on the loan—if you’re lucky, you might be able to find a payment plan with an interest-free option if you pay off the loan within a certain time frame. Also, always make sure that you factor in the interest you’ll be paying and any annual credit card fees before you go ahead with the loan.

Can You Finance Mountain Bikes?

You can finance a mountain bike like you would any other large purchase—with a credit card, a loan, or a layaway arrangement with a store. Mountain bikes can cost thousands of dollars, so it’s no surprise that you’d want to pay for them with a loan.

What is a Good Price to Pay for a Mountain Bike?

There are three different tiers of mountain bike: hardtails, full-suspension bikes, and downhill racers. Expect to pay $400 to $800 for a hardtail, somewhere between $1000 and $2500 for a full-suspension bike, and between $3000 and $10,000 for a downhill racer.

Is Building a Mountain Bike Cheaper than Buying One?

You might think that building your bike would be cheaper than buying it because you’re doing the work of assembling the thing, but this is not the case.

Bike manufacturers have business arrangements with parts makers that allow them to buy parts in bulk, so they’ll always be able to undercut a lone individual.

However, if you’re quite savvy and you play your cards right, you might be able to construct your bike more cheaply by taking advantage of deals on online markets or using spare parts. This option does have the advantage of full customization if you’re willing to put in the work and learn how to build a bike.

Final Thoughts

The price you pay for your mountain bike comes down to how much you’re willing to spend. There’s a large price range, and if you’re willing to take out a loan and pay it down for a long time, you can afford even the higher-end bikes while on a budget.
